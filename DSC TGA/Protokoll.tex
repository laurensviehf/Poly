\section{Theory Part}

    \subsection{DSC}

        The DSC (Differential Scanning Calorimetry) uses the principle of the isotherm calorimetry. The Sample and a references get heated in two separate crucibles. 
        The absorbed energy is measured, and in case of different absorption the one with less gets more power while this added power is being measured and plotted against time (t).
        The Integral of dW/dt is the difference between the sample and the reference, also shown in picture (\ref{DSC_Kurve}) is the Integral underneath the curve which defines the smelting 
        enthalpy which is calculated by using equation (\ref{Enthalpie}). This curve is influenced by the spectrum of lengths the polymer provides, 
        meaning if the polymer has many chains with different lengths the peak gets wider to the right. 
        The reference is always a known Sample or something, the heat capacity is known. 
        In our case the reference was the empty crucible.


    \begin{equation}
            \label{Enthalpie}
            \Delta H_m =  \Delta H_\text{Sample} - \Delta H_\text{Reference}
    \end{equation}    

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \begin{center}
            \includegraphics[scale=0.75]{../Bilder/DSC Integral.png}
            \caption{This picture shows the thermogram of a Polymer, the differential signal in [mV] is plotted against time in [s]. xxx }
            \label{DSC_Kurve}
        \end{center}
    \end{figure}

        Via this melting enthalpy the crystallization grade can be determined by using the melting enthalpy and by using $\Delta H_m^0$. 
        $\Delta H_m^0$ cannot directly be determined, because polymers are not fully crystalline. Therefore, a extrapolation is used to determine the ideal case. Meaning the length of the polymer is imagined to be infinite
        This all results in following equation.

    \begin{equation}
        \alpha = \frac{\Delta H_m}{\Delta H_m^0}
    \end{equation}

    \subsubsection{Glass transition temperature (T$_\text{g}$) and melting temperature (T$_\text{m}$)}

        The Glass transition temperature (T$_\text{m}$) is the temperature range where a complete coupled segment movement of chains (10-15 chain links) is possible. 
        These chains were frozen before that temperature and can only be defined if the polymer is amorphous. This can also be seen in a change of the heat capacity.\\
        
        The melting temperature (T$_\text{g}$) is only present in semi crystalline polymers. These have a lamellar structure(folding crystal) with amorphous parts between the backfolding parts.
        When reaching the smelting temperature these backfolded parts are moving apart. The smelting temperature can be influenced by the amount of amorphous parts in the polymer, meaning if there are more amorphous parts the smelting temperature gets higher.\\
        
        Both of these temperatures can be analyzed and read out of the thermogram. For semi crystalline polymers with a bigger amorphous share the peak is more to the right because of the higher temperature.

                \caption{This picture shows an example of a thermogram of a polymer, in which T$_\text{g}$ is the Glass transition temperature and T$_\text{m}$ is the melting temperature.}

    \subsection{TGA}    
            
            The main principle of TGA (Thermo gravimetry) is the heating of a Sample to the point where it gets destroyed.
            In the process the mass is monitored and plotted against temperature. It is also possible to plot the temperature against the time.
            If these two graphs are compared a clear corelation can be observed.\\
            For analytical purposes the m-T-curve ist the more valuable one because from this curve things like the starting temperature and the end temperature of the degrading can be determined by tangent constructions.
            This can also be observed in picture (\ref{TGA-Kurve}).

            \begin{figure}[H]
                \centering
                \includegraphics[scale = 0.5]{../Bilder/TGA-Kurve.png}

                \label{TGA-Kurve}
            \end{figure}

            Via this diagram the material specific properties can be characterized.

    As can be seen in the attachment on pages \pageref{pdf:ps-1cc} and \pageref{pdf:ps-2hc}, for $it$-PS were no crystallization enthalpy and no 
    second melting enthalpy observed. Thus, before the measurement both polymers are semi-crystalline, however, the chosen cooling conditions of 
    $\SI{20}{\frac{\text{°C}}{min}}$ are too fast for the PS to crystallize again and is therefore amorphous during the measurement of the second 
    heating curve.\\
    A signal can also be observed in the second heating curve of the isotactic polystyrene, which occurs immediately after the glass transition 
    temperature. This may be due to the fact that, during rapid cooling, the chains are aligned parallel to one another, as shown on the left in 
    Figure \ref{möglichekristalline}, but are not crystalline. 
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \centering
            \includegraphics[scale = 0.5]{../Bilder/übergang-amorph-kristallin.pdf}
            \caption{The figure shows possible structural elements of the polymer chains. \cite{wunderlich_structure_2005}} 
            \label{möglichekristalline}
        \end{figure}
    
    Upon reaching the glass transition temperature, the chains move and can align in a partially crystalline manner. This alignment triggers a 
    melting process, which, however, occurs at low temperatures. This is because the length of the crystalline regions is very small, and 
    according to Equation \ref{}, the melting temperature is very low. \cite{wunderlich_structure_2005}

    Using the melting enthalpy, the degree of crystallization $\alpha$ were calculated to
    \begin{align*}
        \alpha_\text{PET} =& \frac{\SI{38.368}{\frac{J}{g}}}{\SI{140}{\frac{J}{g}}} = 0.274 \\
        \alpha_{it\text{-PS}} =& \frac{\SI{24.194}{\frac{J}{g}}}{\SI{87}{\frac{J}{g}}} = 0.278 
    \end{align*}

    As noted previously, the $it$-PS could not crystallize under the cooling rate and therefore became amorphous. As a result, only a glass transition temperature 
    and no melting temperature can be observed in the second heating curve.
